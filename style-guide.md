STYLE GUIDE OVERVIEW
THE PURPOSE OF A STYLE GUIDE
The goal of this style guide is to develop a consistent brand and team image through guidelines encompassing logos, fonts, colors, and their usage.

This style guide describes the framework for how TrickFire Robotics should be represented visually and verbally. Adherence to this style guide lends itself to consistency, recognizability, and professionalism. Additionally, this guide serves to specify style requirements and standards, ensuring a unified image across branding and communication.

MOTIVES FOR REBRANDING
This style guide is a product of TrickFire’s rebranding efforts in June through August of 2025. At this time, the University of Washington Bothell experienced significant growth, leading to both an influx in TrickFire membership as well as competition with other student groups. As a team, we found a need to refocus our public image to accommodate these changes.

Particularly, we hoped to develop a more identifiable brand and address inconsistencies in how TrickFire was represented. TrickFire Robotics has always strived to be welcoming, but our original branding fell short of this friendly tone. While rebranding, we primarily focused on selecting more appropriate fonts along with an additional color that stands out and feels inviting. Throughout this process, we gathered diverse perspectives in order to update our brand to more accurately represent the team.

BREAKING THE RULES
Guidelines are guidelines. If part of this style guide feels too complex or restrictive for an application, a looser interpretation may be acceptable as long as you are prudent and maintain the brand standards to the fullest extent possible. This is a living document, subject to change as we revise content, identify gaps, and discover best practices. The recommendations presented in the ensuing pages can be stretched and modified under good judgement.

BRAND IDENTITY
TrickFire Robotics is a large-scale team at the University of Washington Bothell that competes in the University Rover Challenge, an international competition challenging students to design and build rovers relevant to future Mars exploration.

TrickFire seeks to represent itself as a professional aerospace robotics club while also appearing authentic, welcoming, collaborative, and interdisciplinary.

Our brand strives to illustrate the professional and technical development opportunities students get through the club, as well as the community our student-led team fosters.

CLUB LOGOS
TrickFire Robotics has two official logos, the TrickFire Robotics logo and the TrickFire flame logo. Some sort of TrickFire logo should be included on all promotional materials, documents, posters, slideshows, etc. for the club. Using the full logo is generally preferred. The flame logo can be used in tight spaces or when the club name is provided elsewhere.

GRAYSCALE LOGOS
When a TrickFire logo is used on top of an image, busy background, or in a black and white design, use a grayscale version of the TrickFire Robotics logo or TrickFire flame logo. The grayscale logo should only be used in these specified circumstances. The classic colored logo is preferred and should always be used in lieu of the grayscale logo if it fits the design.

Logos can be accessed at https://tinyurl.com/TrickFireRoboticsAssets.

APPROPRIATE LOGO USAGE
Whenever a TrickFire logo is used, there should be adequate spacing around it and the logo should be fully visible. Moreover, logos should not be rotated, warped/stretched, discolored, or otherwise disfigured. Avoid adding shadows or outlines to logos.

EXCLUSION ZONE
The recommended minimum visible space between any logo and other design elements should be proportional to the letter height of an Overpass uppercase T roughly equivalent to the T in the logo. This exclusion zone should be measured using the full height of the logo from the extremes of the icon (the bottom/top of the flame and the extreme left edge of the R). Sizing recommendations apply to all logo variations.

MINIMUM LOGO SIZE
Ensure the TrickFire logo is visible, legible, and recognizable at all times. The logo should be no smaller than about 0.75”.

MAXIMUM LOGO SIZE
Avoid making the logo so large that it becomes blurry or takes up the entire design. It should not overpower the design.

Use your best judgement to determine the appropriate size for a logo in the specific context, but typically no application of either logo should exceed roughly a third of the design.

STRETCHING THE COLOR SET
In color theory, shades, tints, and tones refer to variations of a base color. These color manipulation techniques are important in design as they enable various visual effects (depth, highlights, shadows, etc.) without altering the base color. Extending the color palette within limits can be a powerful way to build visual interest. Variations of TrickFire Green and Accent Pink can be picked with an online tool.

Color Variation Picker: https://coolors.co/00fe00

SHADES
A shade is a darker version of a base color, achieved by adding black.

TINTS
A tint is a lighter version of a base color, achieved by adding white.

TONES
A tone reduces a color’s intensity and vibrancy, achieved by adding gray (a mix of black and white).
EXPANDING THE COLOR PALETTE

BODY TEXT
For large bodies of text, use Overpass. Longer textual content should generally be White or Dark White on an Eerie Black or Black background or the opposite. Most applications will follow a “dark theme,” with light colored text and icons on a dark surface, but the reverse is also acceptable.

HEADER TEXT
For titles, captions, and headlines, use Barlow Condensed Semi Bold in all capital letters. As headers often involve large or bold text, header text may involve more colors. When a TrickFire Green background is used, make the header text Eerie Black or Black in color. When an Accent Pink background is used, make the header text White in color. Please note that the level of contrast is much more accessible with Eerie Black or Black on TrickFire Green than White on Accent Pink, although the contrast ratio improves as the text gets larger/bolder.

COLOR CONTRAST
If you are unsure how accessible your design is, use a color contrast checker to assess the readability. Good color contrast is essential in ensuring readability, understandability, and accessibility for all. Additionally, high contrast color combinations help individuals with visual impairments or color blindness, affecting how people interact with and navigate the design.
